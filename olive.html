Olive: UX Design Case Study

Project Overview

The Challenge:

Since the beginning of the COVID-19 pandemic, there was an increase in physical and mental health issues among the public, with even harsher effects experienced by immigrants and foreign-born individuals in the U.S. However, through the market research, I found that there were no apps fully dedicated to addressing the needs of this particular user group.

The Goal:

So I chose to tackle this problem that has not received enough attention: design a wellness app that focuses on the needs of foreign-born individuals living in the U.S., helping users to get personalized recommendations on wellness management while undertaking challenges of living in a foreign country during the pandemic.

The Result:

Through 27 user surveys, 3 user interviews, 2 user testings, and 10+ iterations (and ongoing), I completed the conceptual design of Olive, an AI-driven wellness app that provides personalized recommendations to help users stay on top of their health and wellness. Users can discover new activities, track their progress, and invite friends to join the wellness journey all in one place! The prototype has a 4.5 of 5 user satisfaction score based on SEQ survey results.

Design Process

The Design thinking process sets the foundation for the Olive app design. This iterative, user-centered approach enables me to redefine problems from users perspectives and find alternative solutions to address users needs.

EMPATHIZE

Problem Statement

A well-defined problem statement is critical to understand the user goals and set business objectives of a UX Design project.

“The Olive app users need a way to get personalized recommendations on wellness management while undertaking challenges of living in a foreign country during the pandemic because they want to learn how to keep a healthy lifestyle and prevent mental exhaustion.”

Competitor Analysis

To make more informed decisions, competitive analysis on two similar apps, Sanvello and Lifesum, was conducted to assess the current market space.

Key Insights:

  • The majority of apps only focus on one aspect of wellness, physical fitness or mental health.
  • Many apps ask new users to complete a lengthy wellness assessment when they sign-up.
  • Popular apps require subscription fees for full access and paid features are not cost-friendly.
  • None of the existing apps in the U.S. market provide video and audio content in languages other than English.

User Research

To understand user behaviors and uncover user pain points with existing health and wellness apps, I conducted 27 user surveys to collect quantitative data and 3 remote user interviews for qualitative data. Also, affinity map was applied to compile and analyze data, grouping related information into clusters.

Key Insights:

  • Users have irregular work hours since they started working from home so they need to get personalized tips on wellness management for work-life balance.
  • Users experienced loneliness and felt unsupported due to social distancing and being away from their home country.
  • Many gym-goers missed their gym buddies and want to find alternative group workout experiences online.
  • Users are motivated when they can see achievements and progress.
  • Non-native English-speaking users would love to access audio/video resources in their native languages.

DEFINE

User Personas - User Journeys - User Flows

Based on information gathered from user research, two User Personas were created to represent different target user groups for the app. This helps me to make informed design decisions based on real data from real users and reminds me to keep a user-centric mindset at every stage of design.

Then, to have a more in-depth understanding of users' goals and the context of app uses, user journey maps were created to visualize users’ mental models (thoughts, behaviors, feelings) when they try to complete tasks and achieve their goals.

Finally, two user flows were created to show the actual screens that users will interact with when using the Olive app.

IDEATE

Sitemap

To design the information architecture for the app, an initial sitemap was created based on user flows and main functions of the app. Building out the sitemap requires a lot of reiteration, which is one of the most important steps in UX Design for it builds the foundation of the app.

Then, to test the information hierarchies of the sitemap, an open card sorting with 30 cards was conducted using Optimal Workshop. From the result of the similarity matrix, we see a clear pattern of how the participants have grouped various app features with only a few overlapped categories that need to be further simplified.

PROTOTYPE

From Low-Fidelity Wireframes to High-Fidelity Prototype

First, I sketched out low-fidelity wireframes on paper focusing on the navigation hierarchy and main functions that are critical to my target users. This is the best and most efficient way to flesh out ideas and sort out all the important points from the initial user research and sitemap.

Then, I used Balsamiq to create mid-fidelity wireframes corresponding to screens from the task flows that user personas would interact with. Adding interactions to these wireframes, I created the first Mid-fidelity prototype to better convey the app’s navigation and functions.

Finally, I started creating a high-fidelity prototype in AdobeXD, adding colors, typography, visuals, interactions, and accessibility features. Also, I used the iOS Human Interface Guidelines, Google Material Design, a responsive grid system, and a mobile-first approach as reference to follow the industry standards.

Below is a snapshot of three main user flows:

  • Sign up ( Olive, splash, sign up)
  • Discover Screen (main page, fiters, video result)
  • Wellness Check-in ( launch screen, results & recommendation)

TEST

Usability Testing & Preference Test

With the high-fidelity prototype, Olive was ready to be put through the usability testing. A Usability Test Plan was created to streamline the process and 6 remote moderated task-based tests were conducted via zoom, using screen sharing and recording

Click here to see the full test plan.

Through analyzing test results with Affinity Maps and Rainbow Spreadsheet, five major issues were identified based on the numbers and severity of errors:

  • Unclear Navigation - Lack of indicator on the welcome screen to show users the next step
  • UI Misinterpretation - Skip partial onboarding due to “sign up/login” buttons on splash screen
  • Accessibility - The font size is too small to read
  • Improper Information Hierarchy - Users missed the “Start Check-in” button at first attempt
  • Cognitive overload - Too much information on display on the “Me” page

In addition, two preference tests were conducted to find out which Welcome screen is visually more attractive to users and which Sign-up layout is easier to navigate for Olive users.

ITERATE

Polishing the design

In this phase of the project, I made final revisions to the prototype based on user testing results, peer reviews from design collaboration, and Gestalt visual design principles. The new iterations have successfully solved the previous errors from the usability test, improving user satisfaction score (1-5) from 3 to 4.5.

Design Style Guide

Before wrapping up the project, I create a Design Style Guide, including color palette, Typography, UI Patterns, and Tone of voice to standardize my design so that it can be easily shared with stakeholders (designers, developers, Product Manager).
